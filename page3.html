Navigating Climate Anxiety

By Ethan Le





As climate-related events intensify daily, so does the corresponding surge in climate anxiety. Like climate action, easing anxiety about the climate crisis is easier said than done. Climate change is recognized as one of the top threats to global health in the 21st century. Climate anxiety, also known as eco-anxiety, is not merely a byproduct of sensationalized news headlines or apocalyptic predictions. It is a genuine psychological response to the tangible threats climate change poses. The intensification of climate-related events on the news, from wildfires and hurricanes to rising sea levels, contributes to an overwhelming uncertainty about the future. The realization that our planet is undergoing profound, irreversible changes can evoke a deep emotional response, creating a storm within us that is as real as any natural disaster.

The Impact

The effects of climate anxiety extend far beyond concerns for the environment. Sleep disturbances, feelings of helplessness, a persistent sense of impending doom, and without careful management, can seriously affect our overall mental health or even lead to forms of Post Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD). The constant barrage of distressing news and the weight of knowing that future generations may inherit a planet in annarchy contribute to a profound emotional burden. Recognizing and acknowledging these impacts is crucial for fostering a supportive dialogue around mental health in the context of climate change.
The anxiety further deepens when faced with questions of trust and accountability. How can I trust that you will do the right thing? And how can I trust that you trust that I will do the right thing? These concerns can become a significant source of anxiety, causing individuals to feel paralyzed and reluctant to take action, fearing judgment or inadequacy.

How to Cope With Eco-Anxiety

While it's easy to feel like it's your sole responsibility to save the world on your own, try to let go of unnecessary worry by accepting that you can only control what you can control. Reassure yourself that we can achieve a lot if we all do a little. Knowledge is a powerful antidote to anxiety. Educate yourself about climate change, its causes, and potential solutions. Reading articles, watching videos, and enrolling in classes can help immensely. Understanding the issues empowers you to engage in informed conversations and advocate for change. It's good to stay in the loop and be aware of how the climate crisis is unfolding â€“ but it's damaging to fixate all your attention, the news and doom-scroll constantly on social media. It can be more helpful to be more selective regarding your news sources. Instead of tuning into TV stations or media sources like TikTok and Instagram, make an effort to follow trustworthy figures, articles, and videos.
If climate anxiety starts affecting your mental health significantly, consider seeking professional support. Therapists and counselors can provide coping strategies and a safe space to navigate through these complex emotions. As the storm of climate anxiety rapidly changes in our changing world, it's crucial to recognize its impact on mental health and well-being. By acknowledging these feelings, fostering a supportive community, and taking steps to cope, individuals can transform anxiety into a form of positive action.
