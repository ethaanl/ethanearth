The Solution to Global Warming: Beavers?

By Ethan Le





Beavers are the national animal of Canada. While cherished by Canadians, only some are aware of the pivotal role these creatures play in shaping ecosystems and mitigating environmental impacts. As our climate changes in the Pacific Northwest, the importance of beavers becomes increasingly evident. With a projected reduction in summer precipitation and an increase in winter and spring precipitation, the role of beavers as "nature's architects" takes on a new level of significance. Riverscapes are stream or river habitats where beavers reside. Riverscapes with beaver dam complexes can naturally store more water during storms and slowly release it later in the year. This reduces flood peaks and can prolong water availability during prolonged drought.

Keystone Engineers

Beavers are ecosystem engineers that dramatically reshape the physical environment around them. As the climate rapidly changes, it is becoming harder and harder for various animals and plants to adapt and thrive in their native habitat. Beavers, with their dams, stand as crucial players in sustaining ecosystems. Beaver ponds are wetland habitats that can support a rich concentration of animals and plants. Freshwater fish, insects, amphibians, birds, other animals, and plants. They have a significant impact on the biodiversity of an area and create entire ecosystems. They bring wood into the water, providing food and shelter for insects. Those insects become food for other species, including fish and birds. The insides of beaver lodges also provide homes for other animals, such as mink and river otters. Beaver dams and the wetlands they create also improve water quality in streams, replenish underwater aquifers, reduce water shortages from drought, reduce flooding, and reduce erosion of stream banks.

The Pests of Canada?

Human-beaver conflicts have been happening for centuries. In the late 1800s, beavers nearly went extinct in the United States and Canada due to centuries of fur trapping and extermination. More recently, urbanization and agriculture have been encroaching at rapid speeds upon their habitats, threatening these species once again. Beavers are often regarded as annoying pests because of the wetlands they create. When a dam is built in the wrong place, it can flood roadways or homes, and previously, the solution was just to exterminate them.
On the other hand, the messy wetlands they create are amazing for preventing forest fires. As we all know, summers in BC have gotten way hotter over the past few years. The result of this? Forest fires plaguing BC. The wetlands keep the surrounding land wet, and the wetter the ground, the less likely it is to burn. These are called fire breaks; they provide a safe place for animals to go when a fire does break out and prevent the fire from spreading even more.



Extinction Prevention

Many Indigenous communities here in Canada have always known about the positive impacts of beavers. Today, some tribes work with local governments to help restore beaver populations. Humans and beavers are very similar in many aspects, but one part they are way better at is preserving the planet. So, how can humans adapt our environment to include beavers and all that they do going forward?

Wetland Restoration

Protect and restore wetlands and riparian zones, as these areas are essential for beaver habitats. This includes preserving natural vegetation along riverbanks and water bodies. Implement ecosystem-based management practices that prioritize the health of waterways and the surrounding environment. Develop and implement regulations that support beaver conservation and habitat protection. This may include guidelines for construction near beaver habitats and restrictions on trapping or relocating beavers. Collaborate with Indigenous communities to incorporate their traditional knowledge into regulatory frameworks.
Raise awareness about the positive impacts of beavers and their role in maintaining ecological balance. Educate communities about the benefits of coexisting with these animals and dispel myths or misconceptions. Promote understanding of the traditional ecological knowledge held by Indigenous communities regarding the relationships between humans and beavers.
